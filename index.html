HARVARD
HOOPS

The story of how a coach transformed an academy school into a basketball force, overcoming racial barriers and changing long held perceptions along the way.

Read the Treatment ↓

Logline

When former Duke standout Tommy Amaker takes over Harvard basketball, he builds a team through an unorthodox Breakfast Club culture, asking players who question him as a basketball player to believe in something they have never seen work.

Look & Feel

The Story

01

Act One

The film opens in 1987 as young Tommy Amaker leads Duke against North Carolina, poised and in command, before cutting to the silence outside the athletic director's office at Michigan, where he is fired. With few real opportunities ahead, he considers walking away from coaching entirely until an unlikely option appears: Harvard University. After a long period of doubt, he accepts the job, determined to build something different where basketball has never truly mattered.

02

Act Two

At Harvard, Amaker inherits a program with little expectation of success and players skeptical of both the system and their coach. A small Breakfast Club group reshapes his approach to leadership, emphasizing connection and accountability over traditional coaching. As the team finds unexpected momentum, a university-wide academic scandal strikes, sidelining players and fracturing trust — forcing Amaker to confront whether what he is building can hold together.

03

Act Three

With belief fading, Amaker returns to the core principles that started it all. Recommitted to each other, the players push toward history, earning a shot at the Ivy League championship — and securing the program's first title, ending a 65-year NCAA tournament drought. In the final moments, the once-empty gym is filled with purpose, as Amaker watches, no longer defined by his past as a player, but by what he has created.

Tone

Grounded, intelligent, and restrained. Harvard Hoops carries the quiet intensity of Moneyball and The Social Network, where drama is driven by pressure and internal conflict rather than spectacle. The camera lingers in spaces most sports movies avoid — empty gyms, film rooms, classrooms — creating a sense that the real battles are happening off the court.

By the final act, the wins matter, but they are not treated as miracles. They are treated as proof. The ending is earned, grounded, and lasting — less about a single victory, more about the realization that something meaningful has been built.

Key Characters

01

Tommy Amaker

A respected former Duke standout and accomplished coach who has built his career on discipline and relentless work ethic. Composed and confident on the surface, he wrestles with questions about how his own basketball journey is perceived, and whether his greatest achievements are still behind him.

02

The Star Player

A gifted Harvard athlete with the talent to dominate on the court and the intelligence to thrive in the classroom. Confident, sharp, and highly skeptical, he is quietly unraveling under academic pressure and the fear that his potential may never become more than promise.

03

The Mentor

A wise and seasoned elder whose quiet presence commands immediate respect. Thoughtful and unshaken by ego, he sees through Tommy's polished exterior and challenges him to stop chasing validation and start leading from a place of honesty and purpose.

Music & Sound

Sophisticated, emotional, and restrained — modern orchestral textures, subtle piano motifs, and rhythmic percussion building momentum without becoming sentimental. Empty gyms, squeaking sneakers, bouncing basketballs, and campus silence define the world as much as the score itself. By the final act, music feels earned rather than triumphant.

Approach

Camera work mixes intimacy with control — close shots inside practices and personal moments, wide composed frames for Harvard's historic architecture and scale. Lighting leans into contrast and mood, cool tones and dramatic interiors making Harvard feel beautiful, old, and intimidating. Editing favors rhythm and tension, connecting present moments to Breakfast Club conversations that reveal character under pressure.

Casting

Casting is critical to grounding this story in authenticity — actors who convey quiet intensity and internal conflict rather than external spectacle, cast for essence over name recognition, in a process as inclusive as the real Harvard team itself.

Tommy Amaker

Late 40s–50s. Composure with buried self-doubt in the same performance.

The Star Player

A younger actor, ideally with real basketball ability, who can hold academic and athletic pressure onscreen.

The Mentor

A seasoned actor who commands a room through stillness rather than volume.

Location

Set primarily on and around Harvard University's campus in Cambridge, Massachusetts — real academic and athletic spaces rather than a heightened setting. Historic architecture and quiet quads sit alongside the more lived-in spaces of college athletics: practice gyms, film rooms, locker rooms, and the arena on game night. Empty gyms and early-morning campus exteriors recur as visual motifs, reinforcing themes of discipline and the quiet work behind any public success.

Fin.

“Harvard Hoops is about what it means to lead, to doubt yourself, and to build something real when no one expects it to work.”
